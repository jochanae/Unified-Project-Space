## What's Actually Being Solved

The graveyard of home improvement projects isn't a lack of inspiration. Pinterest, Houzz, and Instagram have solved inspiration. The graveyard is what happens after inspiration — the gap between "I want this" and "I know what to do first."

That gap has three specific failure points:

**Visualization failure.** People can't see what their specific space would look like transformed. Generic inspiration images don't translate to their awkward backyard with the weird slope and the existing concrete pad.

**Scope failure.** Projects feel monolithic. "Redesign my backyard" is paralyzing. Nobody knows where to start, what it costs, or what requires a professional versus a Saturday afternoon.

**Execution failure.** Even people who get past the first two hit a wall finding trustworthy contractors, getting realistic quotes, and managing a project they've never managed before.

Your platform addresses all three. That's ambitious. The question is whether one product can credibly solve all three or whether trying to solve all three means solving none of them well.

## The Competitive Landscape — Honest Assessment

This space is more crowded than it appears and less solved than the crowding suggests.

**Houzz** — The incumbent. 40 million monthly users. Strong inspiration, professional directory, some project management tools. Weak on AI visualization and personalization. Feels like a magazine, not a tool.

**Thumbtack and Angi** — Contractor matching with cost estimates. No visualization. No project planning. Pure lead generation dressed as a service.

**DreamzAR and Planner 5D** — AR and 3D room planning tools. Technical and requiring effort. Not conversational. Not photo-first in the way you're describing.

**Midjourney and DALL-E prompting communities** — People are already doing DIY versions of your visualization concept by prompting AI with photos of their spaces. It works poorly, requires technical knowledge, and produces no actionable output beyond an image.

**Havenly and Modsy** — Interior design services using AI-assisted visualization. Modsy shut down in 2022 despite strong product-market fit because unit economics were brutal. Important cautionary data point.

**The honest gap:** Nobody has combined photo-based AI visualization with phased project planning, cost estimation, and contractor connection in a single coherent experience. The pieces exist separately. The integration doesn't.

## The Core User Journey — Where It Works and Where It Breaks

**Photo upload and description** — This works. Multimodal AI can interpret a photo and a plain language description and generate a transformation visualization. The technology exists today.

**Visualization quality** — This is the moment of magic or disappointment. If the generated image looks generic, doesn't respect the actual geometry of the space, or places a water feature where the driveway clearly can't support one, trust breaks immediately. Visualization quality is the product's first impression and its most fragile moment.

**Project phasing** — This is genuinely hard to do well. Breaking "redesign my backyard" into realistic phases requires understanding scope, sequencing dependencies, local permit requirements, and the user's budget and skill level. Done poorly it's useless. Done well it's the most valuable thing the product does.

**Cost estimation** — Notoriously difficult. Material costs vary by region. Labor costs vary enormously by market. Contractor availability affects pricing. Any estimate the platform provides will be wrong for someone, and the question is whether it's usefully directional or dangerously misleading. This is a liability and a trust problem that needs careful design.

**DIY versus professional separation** — Smart feature. People want to know what they can reasonably do themselves. The tricky part is that this varies by user skill level, local permit requirements, and project specifics. A blanket "this is DIY" recommendation that turns out to require a permit creates a bad outcome.

**Contractor connection** — This is where most platforms in this space make their money and lose their soul. Lead generation economics incentivize quantity over quality. The moment the platform prioritizes contractor revenue over user outcomes, the product breaks.

## Target Customer Profiles

**The Inspired Homeowner**
Recently bought a home or recently decided to invest in their existing one. Has clear aesthetic preferences but no project management experience. Overwhelmed by where to start. Will pay for clarity and confidence. This is your primary user.

**The Weekend DIYer**
Capable and motivated. Wants visualization and material lists more than contractor connections. Values the platform as a planning tool. Lower monetization potential per user but high engagement and strong word of mouth.

**The Overwhelmed Upgrader**
Has a specific trigger — selling the house, hosting an event, neighbor envy — that creates urgency. Needs to move fast and will pay for professional help. Highest monetization potential. Shortest planning window.

**The Contractor**
Your supply-side customer if you build a marketplace. Quality contractors are the scarcest resource in home improvement. Acquiring good ones and keeping them on the platform is harder than acquiring homeowners.

## The Business Model Question — This Needs to Be Decided Early

There are three fundamentally different businesses hiding inside your concept:

**Business A — SaaS visualization and planning tool**
Users pay a subscription for AI visualization, project planning, and cost estimation. No contractor marketplace. Clean unit economics. Limited ceiling.

**Business B — Contractor marketplace with planning tools**
Free planning tools drive homeowner acquisition. Revenue comes from contractor leads or transaction fees. Higher ceiling. Harder to build. Quality control is the permanent challenge.

**Business C — Managed project service**
The platform doesn't just connect you to contractors — it manages the project. Full service. Highest value. Requires operational infrastructure that's expensive to build and hard to scale.

Most platforms in this space start with A, try to build B, and discover that B's economics require compromises that break A's user experience. Deciding which business you're building before you build anything is more important than most of the product decisions that follow.

## The Hardest Problem Nobody Talks About

Contractor quality and reliability is the unsolved problem in every home improvement platform that has ever existed.

Angi has been trying to solve it for 25 years. Thumbtack has been trying for 15. Neither has cracked it. The reason is structural — good contractors don't need lead generation platforms because they have more work than they can handle from referrals. The contractors who need the platform are disproportionately the ones you don't want on the platform.

If your product connects users to contractors, this is the problem you're inheriting. It doesn't mean you shouldn't build it. It means you need a specific, honest answer to "how do we ensure contractor quality" before you launch that feature.

## Risks Worth Naming Now

**Visualization gap between expectation and reality.** If the AI image shows a stunning transformation and the finished project looks nothing like it, you own the disappointment even if you didn't build the project.

**Cost estimate liability.** Estimates that are significantly wrong — in either direction — damage trust and create real financial harm for users who budget based on them.

**Contractor marketplace quality death spiral.** Bad contractor experiences drive users away. Fewer users means less platform value for good contractors. Good contractors leave. Quality declines further.

**The inspiration-to-action gap is emotional, not informational.** Giving someone a beautiful visualization and a detailed plan doesn't guarantee they act. The psychological barriers to starting a home improvement project — cost anxiety, decision paralysis, fear of disruption — aren't solved by better information. The product needs to address motivation, not just planning.

**Big tech adjacency.** Google, Apple, and Amazon all have adjacencies to this space. A Google Maps integration that shows AI-transformed street views of your home is not a far-fetched product extension for Google. You're not competing with them today. You might be in three years.

## MVP Recommendation

Don't build the full platform first. The visualization feature is the hook. Everything else is downstream of whether the visualization creates genuine delight and a desire to act.

**The MVP:**
Photo upload plus plain language description generates a high-quality AI visualization of the transformation. User receives the image plus a rough three-phase project breakdown and a ballpark cost range. No contractor connection. No materials list. No app required — web-based, mobile-optimized.

**What this MVP tests:**
Does the visualization create enough delight that people share it, save it, and want to act on it? If yes, you have the core engagement loop. If no, no amount of planning features will save the product.

**Price point to test:** Free for one visualization. Paid subscription for unlimited visualizations, detailed phasing, and cost estimates. This separates tire-kickers from motivated homeowners and funds iteration.

**The MVP validation question:**
Do users share their visualization without being asked to?

Organic sharing is the signal. It means the visualization is delightful enough to show someone else. That's the product working.

## The Ideal User Journey

**Step 1 — The Photo**

User takes or uploads a photo of their space. Single image. No account required yet. The barrier to entry is as close to zero as possible.

This is the moment of commitment. The product should feel like it's about to do something remarkable with that photo, not like it's asking for information before it does anything useful.

**Step 2 — The Description**

A single open text field. Plain language. No dropdowns, no categories, no forced structure.

*"I want to add a pergola and outdoor kitchen to my backyard"*

That's enough. The platform reads the photo and the description together. The AI understands the space — its dimensions, existing features, visible constraints — and interprets the intent.

One design principle here: don't make the user do the thinking. The platform should ask clarifying questions only when the answer changes the output meaningfully. Location is one of those questions. Budget range is another. Everything else should be inferred or offered as an edit after the fact.

**Step 3 — The Visualization**

This is the product's make-or-break moment.

A high-quality AI-generated image of the transformed space — not a generic backyard with a pergola, but *their* backyard with a pergola positioned relative to their actual fence line, their existing concrete pad, their visible sightlines.

The visualization should feel like someone who actually looked at their space designed this — not like a stock photo was composited onto their photo.

Two to three variations, not one. One feels like a guess. Three feels like design thinking. The user picks the direction that resonates.

**Step 4 — The Reality Layer**

This is what separates the product from a novelty AI image generator.

Beneath the visualization, the user sees:

- **Project phases** — three to four concrete phases with names that feel actionable, not generic. Not "Phase 1: Planning." More like "Phase 1: Clear the space and prep the ground — 1 weekend, ~$200–400."
- **Rough total cost range** — not a precise estimate, a range with an honest explanation of what drives the variance. Location, material quality, whether they hire out or DIY.
- **DIY versus professional split** — a clear, simple breakdown. What they can reasonably do themselves. What requires a licensed professional. What's optional to DIY but faster with help.
- **First action** — one specific thing they can do this week to start. Not a call to action for the platform. An actual first step.

**Step 5 — The Beginning**

The user saves their plan, shares it, or just closes the tab with more confidence than they had when they opened it.

Version 1 doesn't need to track their progress. It doesn't need to send them reminders. It doesn't need to become their project management tool.

It needs to get them past the paralysis. That's the whole job.

**First action callout**
One specific thing the user can do this week. This is the product's most underrated feature. It converts a plan into momentum.

**Save and share**
Email the plan to yourself. Share the visualization as an image. Both serve the same function — making the plan feel real and persistent.

### Distractions in Version 1

**Contractor connection**
You said no marketplace. Correct call. Even a soft version — "find contractors near you" — pulls the product toward a business model before the core experience is validated.

Useful. Not version 1. The product's job is to get users to start, not to follow them through execution.

## The Foundational Decisions That Need Answers Before Building

These aren't branding or product vision questions. These are architecture decisions. Getting them wrong costs weeks.

---

**Decision 1 — Visualization approach**

Two viable paths:

**Path A — Image-to-image transformation**
User uploads a photo. The AI transforms it directly, preserving the spatial geometry of the actual space. More technically complex. Higher fidelity to the real space. This is the version that makes people say "that's actually my yard."

**Path B — Reference-based generation**
User uploads a photo. The AI uses it as a reference for style and context but generates a new image that approximates the space. Faster to build. Lower spatial fidelity. Risks looking generic.

Path A is the right product. Path B is the faster prototype. You need to decide which you're building for version 1 — because the answer determines your AI infrastructure choices.

My read: **Path A is required** for the product to deliver on its promise. Path B validates the interface but not the core value. If the visualization doesn't feel like their space, the reality check layer beneath it loses credibility.

---

**Decision 2 — AI infrastructure**

The visualization pipeline needs a decision on which model or combination of models handles the transformation.

The current realistic options are Stable Diffusion with ControlNet for spatial preservation, DALL-E 3 for quality and API simplicity, or a combination approach where one model handles depth/structure analysis and another handles generation.

This decision affects cost per generation, output quality, iteration speed, and how much infrastructure you're managing.

You don't need to decide the exact stack right now, but you need to decide **whether you're building on an existing API or running your own model pipeline**. The first is faster and more expensive per generation at scale. The second is slower to build and cheaper at scale.

For version 1, API-first is almost certainly correct.

---

**Decision 3 — Input model for cost estimation**

Cost estimation without a data model behind it is guesswork with false confidence. That's worse than no estimate.

The minimum viable approach uses three inputs: project type, rough square footage or scope, and location. Against those three inputs, the platform produces a range — not a number — derived from regional construction cost indexes that are publicly available.

The decision here is whether version 1 uses a **static regional cost model** (simpler, less accurate, faster to build) or a **dynamic one that queries real data** (more accurate, more complex, dependency on external data sources).

For version 1: static model with honest variance ranges. Present it as a directional guide, not a quote. The language around the estimate matters as much as the estimate itself.

---

**Decision 4 — Account model**

The product can be used without an account. Account creation is optional, at the end, for saving.

But you need to decide what "saving" means in version 1.

Option A — Email delivery only. No persistent account. User emails the plan to themselves. Zero auth complexity.

Option B — Lightweight account. Email plus password or magic link. Saves projects to a dashboard. Enables future features like revision history and progress tracking.

Option A is faster to build and removes all friction from the first session. Option B sets up a relationship with the user that makes every subsequent feature easier to deliver.

My read: **Option B is worth the investment** even in version 1, because a saved project is the mechanism that converts a one-time user into a returning one. But the account creation must happen after the value is delivered — not before.

---

**Decision 5 — Mobile versus web**

This product starts with a photo. That means mobile is the primary context of use for most users — they're standing in their backyard looking at the space they want to transform.

But building a native app creates distribution friction. App store approval, install barriers, and a longer build cycle.

The right version 1 is a **mobile-first web application**. Progressive Web App if you want to enable camera access without an app install. This gets the product in front of users fastest while respecting that the photo-capture moment is inherently mobile.
